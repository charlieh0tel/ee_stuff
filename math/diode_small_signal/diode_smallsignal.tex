The current--voltage characteristic of an ideal p--n junction diode is given by the Shockley equation:
\begin{equation}
\ID = \IS \left( \eu^{\frac{\VD}{n \VT}} - 1 \right).
\label{eq:shockley}
\end{equation}
For forward bias well above the thermal voltage --- specifically, when the exponent $\VD/n\VT$ is large enough that the unity term contributes negligibly to $\ID$ ($\VD \gtrsim 4n\VT$ gives an error of about \SI{2}{\percent}; for $n\approx 1$ this is roughly $\VD \gtrsim \SI{100}{\milli\volt}$, rising to $\sim$\SI{191}{\milli\volt} for Si signal diodes with $n \approx 1.85$) ---
\begin{equation}
\ID \approx \IS\, \eu^{\frac{\VD}{n \VT}},
\label{eq:exp-approx}
\end{equation}
where $\IS$ is the reverse saturation current, $n$ is the ideality factor, and $\VT = kT/q \approx \SI{25.85}{\milli\volt}$ at $T = \SI{300}{\kelvin}$. The factor of $n$ in the validity bound matters: for a Si signal diode with $n \approx 1.85$, this is a $\sim$\SI{90}{\milli\volt} correction relative to assuming $n=1$, and it propagates into every regime boundary below.

Decompose the applied voltage into a DC bias $\VQ$ and a time-varying perturbation $\vd(t)$:
\begin{equation}
\VD = \VQ + \vd.
\end{equation}
Substituting and factoring,
\begin{equation}
\ID = \underbrace{\IS\, \eu^{\frac{\VQ}{n \VT}}}_{\displaystyle \IQ}\, \eu^{\frac{\vd}{n \VT}} = \IQ\, \eu^{\frac{\vd}{n \VT}}.
\end{equation}
We define the Q-point current under the forward-exponential approximation as $\IQ \equiv \IS\, \eu^{\VQ/n\VT}$; the exact Shockley value differs by $\IS$ (the $-1$ term in \eqref{eq:shockley}), negligible at the forward biases considered here. The Maclaurin series of the exponential converges for all $\vd$; the practical question is how many terms are needed for a given accuracy. Expanding,
\begin{equation}
\ID = \IQ \left[ 1 + \frac{\vd}{n \VT} + \frac{1}{2!}\left(\frac{\vd}{n \VT}\right)^{\!2} + \frac{1}{3!}\left(\frac{\vd}{n \VT}\right)^{\!3} + \cdots \right].
\end{equation}
The AC component is $\id \equiv \ID - \IQ$. Defining the small-signal conductance at the Q-point as $\gd \equiv \IQ/(n \VT) = 1/\rd$, this becomes
\begin{equation}
\id = \gd \vd + \frac{\gd}{2\, n \VT}\, \vd^{\,2} + \frac{\gd}{6\,(n \VT)^2}\, \vd^{\,3} + \cdots.
\label{eq:power-series}
\end{equation}

\pdfbookmark[1]{Distortion and regime boundaries}{distortion}
\subsection*{Distortion and regime boundaries}

For a sinusoidal drive $\vd = \vhd \cos\omega t$, each term in \eqref{eq:power-series} generates harmonics of $\omega$. The fractional second- and third-harmonic distortions relative to the fundamental are
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{HD2} \approx \frac{\vhd}{4\, n \VT}, \qquad
\mathrm{HD3} \approx \frac{\vhd^{\,2}}{24\,(n \VT)^2}.
\end{equation}
These quantitative criteria, rather than $\VT$ itself, set the boundaries between operating regimes. As the drive amplitude grows, the diode traverses four regimes in turn --- linear, quadratic / square-law, intermediate exponential, and full-Shockley / rectifying --- each treated in its own subsection below.

\subsection*{Linear regime}

For $\vhd \lesssim 0.04\, n\VT$ ($\sim$\SI{1}{\milli\volt} for $n \approx 1$, $\sim$\SI{1.9}{\milli\volt} for $n \approx 1.85$), HD2 stays below \SI{1}{\percent} and only the leading term of \eqref{eq:power-series} survives:
\begin{equation}
\id \approx \gd \vd.
\label{eq:linear}
\end{equation}
This is the small-signal model used for biasing analysis and dynamic-resistance ($\rd = 1/\gd$) calculations; no appreciable mixing or rectification occurs to first order.

\subsection*{Quadratic / square-law regime}

For $0.04\, n\VT \lesssim \vhd \lesssim n\VT$, HD2 grows from \SI{1}{\percent} to \SI{25}{\percent} and HD3 reaches about \SI{4}{\percent} at the upper edge, but the cubic and higher terms in \eqref{eq:power-series} remain small enough to drop:
\begin{equation}
\id \approx \gd \vd + \frac{\gd}{2\, n \VT}\, \vd^{\,2}.
\label{eq:quadratic}
\end{equation}
The $\vd^{\,2}$ term is the workhorse nonlinearity for resistive mixing: in a two-tone drive at $\omega_1$ and $\omega_2$ it generates sum- and difference-frequency products $\omega_1 \pm \omega_2$, the operating principle of RF mixers, microwave power detectors, and intermodulation tests.

\emph{Terminology note.} The diode is not a true square-law device: the linear conductance $\gd$ is always present and dominates for $\vhd \ll n\VT$. ``Square-law mixing'' refers to the fact that the $\vd^{\,2}$ coefficient is the largest nonlinearity producing sum- and difference-frequency products in a two-tone drive, not to the I--V being purely quadratic.

\subsection*{Intermediate exponential regime}

For drive amplitudes that exceed the quadratic regime but do not yet swing the diode below the validity of the forward-bias approximation ($n \VT < \vhd \lesssim \VQ - 4n\VT$), the power series in \eqref{eq:power-series} converges too slowly to be useful, yet the exponential approximation \eqref{eq:exp-approx} still holds throughout the cycle. This interval is nonempty only when $\VQ > 5n\VT$; otherwise the forward-exponential approximation has already failed before a distinct intermediate regime can appear. The natural representation is the closed-form expansion of the full exponential.

Substituting $\vd = \vhd \cos\omega t$ into \eqref{eq:exp-approx} and letting $x \equiv \vhd / (n \VT)$,
\begin{equation}
\ID(t) \approx \IQ\, \eu^{\,x \cos\omega t}.
\end{equation}
The modified Jacobi--Anger identity expands this as a Fourier series in $\omega t$ with coefficients given by the modified Bessel functions of the first kind, $I_k$:
\begin{equation}
\ID(t) \approx \IQ \left[\, I_0(x) + 2 \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} I_k(x)\, \cos(k \omega t) \,\right].
\label{eq:bessel-expansion}
\end{equation}
Two features of this regime are worth noting:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The diode generates a rich spectrum of harmonics from a single tone purely through exponential nonlinearity, with \emph{no clipping involved}. The DC component is $\IQ\, I_0(x)$ and the peak amplitude of the $k$-th harmonic is $2\IQ\, I_k(x)$ for $k \geq 1$. For fixed $k$ and large $x$, $I_k(x)/I_0(x) \to 1$, so many low-order harmonics acquire comparable Bessel coefficients --- but the asymptotic is not uniform over arbitrarily high $k$ (for $k \gtrsim x$, $I_k(x)$ rolls off rapidly, so the spectrum is not literally flat).
    \item The average current rises with drive amplitude: $\langle \ID \rangle = \IQ\, I_0(x)$, and $I_0(x) > 1$ for $x > 0$. This rectified DC component (and the self-bias shift it produces when the diode feeds a resistive load through a coupling capacitor) is the operating principle of large-signal envelope detectors and grid-leak / cathode-bias detectors.
\end{enumerate}

For $x \ll 1$, $I_0(x) \approx 1 + x^2/4$, $I_1(x) \approx x/2$, and $I_2(x) \approx x^2/8$, recovering the DC shift, fundamental term, and second-harmonic amplitude of the quadratic-regime expansion. For fixed $k$ and $x \gg 1$, $I_k(x) \sim \eu^x/\sqrt{2\pi x}$ up to $k$-dependent corrections, while orders $k \gtrsim x$ still roll off rapidly; truncating the harmonic series at any finite order is no longer meaningful, which is the large-signal behavior encountered as the rectifying regime is approached (rectification itself being set by $\vhd$ relative to $\VQ$, not by $x$ alone).

\subsection*{Full-Shockley / rectifying regime}

When $\vhd \gtrsim \VQ - 4n\VT$, the negative half-cycle drives $\VD$ below the validity range of the forward-bias approximation \eqref{eq:exp-approx} and the full Shockley equation \eqref{eq:shockley} is required. Three distinct sub-thresholds appear within this regime:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vhd \approx \VQ - 4n\VT$: the exponential approximation begins to lose instantaneous accuracy at the trough of the swing. The current waveform may already be highly asymmetric, but conduction remains finite throughout the cycle; no reverse-biased interval has appeared yet.
    \item $\vhd \approx \VQ$: the trough first reaches $\VD = 0$. A reverse-biased interval appears for any larger swing, but the waveform only just touches zero; the diode is not yet a hard rectifier.
    \item $\vhd \approx \VQ + 4n\VT$: the trough first reaches $\VD \lesssim -4n\VT$, where the diode is essentially at its reverse-saturation floor $\ID \to -\IS$. For still larger swings, the reverse-biased interval expands and the waveform approaches half-wave rectification.
\end{itemize}

The Bessel expansion \eqref{eq:bessel-expansion} does \emph{not} cease to be a useful description at the first threshold. For the ideal Shockley equation, the Jacobi--Anger expansion remains mathematically exact: substituting $\VD = \VQ + \vhd\cos\omega t$ into \eqref{eq:shockley} gives
\begin{equation}
\ID(t) = \IS\,\eu^{\VQ/n\VT}\left[I_0(x) + 2\sum_{k=1}^\infty I_k(x)\cos(k\omega t)\right] - \IS,
\label{eq:bessel-full-shockley}
\end{equation}
so the $-1$ term in \eqref{eq:shockley} reappears as the $-\IS$ DC offset of \eqref{eq:bessel-full-shockley} and leaves every harmonic amplitude $2\IQ I_k(x)$ unchanged. In practice, however, the large-signal rectifying regime is rarely well-described by a prescribed sinusoidal $\vd$: as $\vhd$ approaches and exceeds $\VQ$, the diode voltage is shaped by circuit effects (load impedance, charge-storage time constants, series resistance, breakdown), and these typically dominate over the closed-form Bessel result. The Bessel expansion remains a useful ideal reference case for a prescribed sinusoidal diode voltage, but the actual waveform in a real rectifier circuit must be solved with full Shockley device physics plus the embedding network. Table~\ref{tab:diode-regimes} and Figure~\ref{fig:regimes} summarize the four operating regimes and their boundaries.

The framework above assumes a robust forward bias ($\VQ > 5n\VT$), so the forward-exponential approximation \eqref{eq:exp-approx} defines a natural expansion point and the $-1$ in \eqref{eq:shockley} contributes only the DC offset noted above. Near $\VQ = 0$ --- the operating point of low-level Schottky detectors --- that approximation fails and the $-1$ becomes the dominant source of curvature; a zero-bias analysis Taylor-expands the full Shockley law about $\VD = 0$ rather than the forward-exponential approximation about $\VQ$, yielding its own threshold structure not captured by the regime boundaries derived here.

\subsection*{Numerical values for common small-signal diodes}

The framework above instantiates differently for each diode technology. $\VT = kT/q$ depends only on temperature, so the device-specific variation enters through the ideality factor $n$ (which sets the square-law boundary $n\VT$) and the quiescent forward voltage $\VQ$ (which sets the intermediate-regime upper edge $\VQ - 4n\VT$). Two observations follow. First, the Si p--n signal diode has roughly $1.7\times$ the square-law amplitude headroom of Ge or Schottky devices, simply because its ideality factor is higher at low bias currents; a 1N4148 can tolerate a junction-voltage amplitude up to $\sim$\SI{48}{\milli\volt} before HD2 exceeds \SI{25}{\percent}, versus $\sim$\SI{27}{\milli\volt} for a 1N34A. (In a real mixer the source amplitude that produces a given junction-voltage swing depends on embedding impedance, LO bias, matching network, and conduction angle, so these numbers are upper bounds on the junction excursion, not on the available source power.) Second, the lower $\VQ$ of Ge and Schottky devices means the unclipped Bessel regime is correspondingly narrow: the intermediate-regime upper edge sits near \SI{91}{\milli\volt} for Ge (about \SI{64}{\milli\volt} beyond the $n\VT$ square-law boundary), whereas for a Si p--n diode the upper edge is near \SI{359}{\milli\volt} (about \SI{311}{\milli\volt} of unclipped exponential operation beyond the square-law boundary). (Without the factor of $n$ in the boundary, the Si p--n edge would be misstated by nearly \SI{90}{\milli\volt}; the correction is significant for high-$n$ devices.) This explains the historical division of labor: Ge (and later zero-bias Si Schottky) for sensitive weak-signal detection where low $\VQ$ and low $n$ matter most, and Si p--n diodes for switching, clamping, and mixer service where the wider operating range is useful.

\subsection*{Temperature dependence and detector stability}

The boundaries in Table~\ref{tab:diode-numbers} are room-temperature values. Two distinct temperature effects shift them, and the two act on different boundaries. First, $\VT = kT/q$ scales linearly with absolute temperature; across the industrial range (\SI{-40}{\degreeCelsius} to \SI{85}{\degreeCelsius}) it varies from about \SI{20}{\milli\volt} to \SI{31}{\milli\volt}, stretching the linear and square-law cutoffs ($0.04\,n\VT$ and $n\VT$) by roughly $\pm\SI{20}{\percent}$. Second, the quiescent voltage $\VQ$ at constant $\IQ$ has a strongly negative temperature coefficient: about \SI{-2}{\milli\volt\per\kelvin} for a Si p--n junction, \SI{-1.7}{\milli\volt\per\kelvin} for Ge, and a device- and current-dependent value of comparable magnitude for Schottky devices. The two effects compound on the intermediate-regime upper edge $\VQ - 4n\VT$: $\VQ$ drops at $\sim$\SI{-2}{\milli\volt\per\kelvin} while $4n\VT$ rises at $4n\cdot k/q \approx \SI{+0.64}{\milli\volt\per\kelvin}$ for $n=1.85$ (or about \SI{+0.34}{\milli\volt\per\kelvin} for $n\approx 1$). The boundary therefore drifts at roughly \SI{-2.64}{\milli\volt\per\kelvin} for a Si p--n diode --- about \SI{30}{\percent} faster than the bare $\VQ$ drift, not twice as fast; for Ge and Schottky devices the additional $\VT$ contribution is smaller and the drift is closer to \SI{20}{\percent} faster than $\VQ$ alone.

The practical consequence is that the diode's bias point drifts substantially over temperature, and a fixed-bias envelope detector or RF-to-DC converter reads its own bias drift directly as a baseline shift at the output. Extrapolating a \SI{-2}{\milli\volt\per\kelvin} coefficient from a \SI{0.55}{\volt} reference at \SI{25}{\degreeCelsius}, a Si signal diode at $\IQ = \SI{100}{\micro\ampere}$ moves from $\VQ \approx \SI{0.68}{\volt}$ at \SI{-40}{\degreeCelsius} to $\VQ \approx \SI{0.43}{\volt}$ at \SI{85}{\degreeCelsius} --- a \SI{250}{\milli\volt} baseline shift that swamps the small-signal rectified output the detector is trying to measure. (Real devices show some moderation of the coefficient away from room temperature; the linear extrapolation is a rough upper bound.) Three standard mitigations are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item AC-couple the detector output through a series capacitor, which rejects the slow drift when the wanted signal is time-varying (envelope/audio detection) but is \emph{not} applicable to true RF-to-DC power detectors where the DC level itself is the measurement.
    \item Bias from a current source so that $\IQ$ and hence $\gd$ are better controlled, reducing gain drift even though the diode voltage $\VQ(T)$ still moves.
    \item Use a matched diode pair in a balanced (back-to-back or bridge) topology so the bias-point drift appears as common-mode and cancels, leaving only the signal-dependent rectified component at the differential output.
\end{enumerate}
Many precision microwave detector ICs combine matched diode (or transistor) detector cells with current-source biasing and on-die temperature compensation, so residual error is specified directly in dB over temperature rather than referred back to the underlying $\VQ$ drift.
